### How He Thinks
- **Analytical first.** He processes through numbers, frameworks, and risk scenarios. He doesn't shoot from the hip.
- **Risk-averse by nature.** His KPMG background means he sees the downside first. This isn't obstruction — it's how he's wired.
- **Not a startup person.** He doesn't have experience in the chaos and speed of early-stage. He applies large-company governance thinking to a 7-person startup. This creates friction.
- **Thorough over fast.** He will over-analyze things Steve thinks are obvious. This frustrates Steve. It's also what catches the things Steve would shoot past.
- **Brief and direct in writing.** His emails are short. He uses bullets. He ends with "C." or "Charlie." He doesn't pad.
- **Engaged, not passive.** He's not a rubber-stamp board chair. He actively co-strategizes, pushes back, and has opinions.

### What Triggers His Analytical Mode
- Equity and cap table decisions
- Governance and board process
- Cash management and runway
- Legal exposure (TechDome, contracts, employment classification)
- Anything that could create IRS, SEC, or fiduciary liability

### What He Responds Well To
- Clear framing: here's where we are, here's the decision, here's what I recommend
- Objective criteria: market standards, what Scale/Carta recommends, comparable companies
- Being asked for his expertise — not just his approval
- Short emails with a clear ask at the end
- Being included early, not surprised

### What Shuts Him Down
- Being presented with a fait accompli
- Vague or undefined terms ("we'll figure it out")
- Skipping process steps he considers foundational (409A, board consent, legal review)
- Feeling like he's being managed rather than consulted

## The Dynamic with Steve

### Steve's Anxiety Pattern
Steve has an inner child wound ("not smart enough") that activates in analytical conversations with Charlie. When Charlie goes deep on numbers or process, the old voice says: *you can't keep up with a KPMG partner.*

This is a ULP (Upper Limiting Problem — The Big Leap). The "fun guy" mask can show up as deflection when the conversation gets heavy.

**The reframe:** Steve doesn't need to out-analyze Charlie. He needs to understand interests and bring his own genius (vision, relationships, market intuition) to the table. These are not lesser — they're different.

### The Healthy Dynamic
Charlie and Steve are in a negotiation, but a collaborative one. Charlie's analytical rigor + Steve's vision and bias for action = stronger than either alone. The goal isn't to win arguments — it's to land in the right place for the company.

**Getting to Yes framework:** Focus on interests, not positions. Charlie's conservative stance reflects legitimate risk management interests, not obstruction.

## Voice Rules — Learned from Corrections

### Openers
- **Never**: "Your notes helped me get clearer" — corny, weak, doesn't sound like Steve
- **Never**: "I think we're aligned on mostly everything" — implies disagreement, sounds hedging
- **Never**: "Good exchange" — too polished, too formal
- **Right**: Dive straight in. "Hey Charlie, here are my comments and feedback. Let's nail it down Thursday."
- Steve's natural opener: short, direct, no setup. Gets to the point in sentence one.

### Don't Overcomplicate
- When Steve says something simple and direct, use it. Don't rewrite it into something more "sophisticated."
- If he says "I think we're on the same page — let's nail it down Thursday" — that IS the line. Use it.
- Steve will correct over-polished language. Trust the simple version.

### People's Names in Emails
- Don't add names unnecessarily. "Dan and Leo" in a Moak Casey reference — not needed. "Moak Casey" is sufficient.
- Only name people if the email is going to them or they're the specific subject.

### Don't Editorialize
- Don't add context Charlie didn't raise (e.g., Patrick's cliff) without checking with Steve first
- Don't put things in a reply that Charlie didn't bring up — that's editorializing, not responding
- Steve will catch it and flag it

### "We" vs "I"
- When something involves a joint commitment between Steve and Charlie, use "we" — e.g., "we should talk to Jason" not "I want to talk to Jason"
- Steve catches this — it signals who owns the action

### Avoiding False Urgency
- Don't set expectations Steve hasn't agreed to — "I want written consent moving the week after Thursday" was too strong
- Let Steve set the pace: "Once we're aligned Thursday, let's get Scale engaged and keep things moving"
